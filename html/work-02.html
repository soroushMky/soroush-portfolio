MediFlow — Trust, Not Just Speed, in AI Clinical Documentation

A concept clinical-documentation tool for therapists and physicians, designed around one constraint: an AI-generated note is only useful if the clinician signing it can verify it faster than they could write it from scratch.

— The Decision That Defines This Project

AI scribes sell themselves on speed. I designed MediFlow around the opposite priority: every AI-generated line had to be falsifiable by the clinician signing it, not just fast to click through. Speed that comes from clinicians stopping to actually verify, not from them giving up on verification, is the only kind of speed that's safe to ship in a clinical record.

01 The Problem

A clinician remains legally and ethically accountable for whatever they sign, AI-drafted or not. A black-box AI note creates a bad fork: either the clinician re-verifies everything line by line — which erases the time savings the tool was supposed to provide — or they stop verifying and start rubber-stamping, which is a liability and patient-safety risk hiding behind a confidence percentage.

MediFlow's entire interaction model exists to avoid that fork: make verification fast enough that clinicians actually do it, every time.

02 Draft Review — Verification at the Line Level

Instead of one aggregate "92% confident" badge sitting over an entire note, every SOAP section — Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan — carries its own status. Each line links to a timestamped source quote from the session audio, so the clinician isn't asked to trust the model's summary — they're shown exactly what the patient said and can judge the leap themselves.

Lines that need a second look are flagged explicitly ("Needs review" on the Assessment line in the screenshot below) rather than buried in a single score. A visible progress meter — 3 of 4 lines, 75% confirmed — turns "did I actually check this" into something the clinician can see, not something they have to remember.

03 Sign-off — A Deliberate Last Step

Once every line is confirmed, the note moves to sign-off — a separate screen, not a single extra click on draft review. Billing, medication reconciliation, and follow-up scheduling are shown as a checklist alongside the finalized note, with the signature itself left as the one item still pending until the clinician deliberately confirms.

"Modify draft" and "Confirm & sign off" are kept as two clearly separate actions, not a single "approve" button — a legal clinical record shouldn't be one accidental tap away from being locked.

04 Note Filed — Where the Speed Actually Shows Up

Trust is front-loaded into draft review and sign-off; the payoff shows up here. This session closed out in 4m 12s against the clinician's own average of 6m 45s — a 38% reduction — without skipping a single verification step. The filed note carries a reference ID, document type, and any carried-over action items for next time, so nothing from this session gets silently dropped before the next visit.

05 The Rest of the Workflow

A clinical tool isn't just the AI moment — it's also the patient's history and the clinician's day. Patient profile surfaces appointment history, tracked problems (clearly separated into Resolved vs. Ongoing), active prescriptions, and a pre-visit notes field so context is loaded before the session starts, not reconstructed during it.

Scheduling keeps the same plain, checklist-style language as the rest of the app — today's appointments, available slots, and a waitlist — rather than introducing a separate visual language for a screen the clinician might only glance at between sessions.

06 Key UX Decisions

Why per-line confidence instead of one score? A single 92% tells a clinician nothing about where to spend their attention. Four scores tell them exactly which line to slow down on.

Why timestamped source quotes? "Trust the AI" isn't an option in a clinical record — "verify against the transcript in two seconds" is. The source quote turns every AI claim into something falsifiable instead of something asserted.

Why lock the note after sign-off? Real clinical documentation needs an audit trail. Once a note is signed, it should behave like a signed document — editable only through an explicit amendment path, not a quiet inline edit.

Why split "Modify draft" from "Confirm & sign off"? The cost of an accidental finalize on a legal medical record is much higher than the cost of one extra click — the UI should reflect that asymmetry.

07 What I Learned
01
Trust is a UX problem before it's a model problem. Line-level verification redistributes trust across a note instead of asking a clinician for blanket faith in one number.
02
Ambiguity costs more in healthcare UI than almost anywhere else. Every status needed an explicit label — "Needs review," "Confirmed," "Signature pending" — color alone wasn't an acceptable signal.
03
The supporting screens carry real weight. Patient profile and scheduling aren't the exciting part of an "AI scribe" pitch, but a tool that nails draft review and neglects them isn't actually usable in a real clinical day.
08 Roadmap

Next: a structured amendment flow for signed notes (rather than re-opening the original), real FHIR-aligned data modeling for patient records, an accessibility pass aimed specifically at clinical environments, and user testing with practicing clinicians to validate whether the verification model actually holds up against real documentation habits — not just against my own assumptions.
